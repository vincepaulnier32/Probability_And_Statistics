    Measures of Central Tendency & Fundamental Counting Principles

    Measures of Central Tendency

    Measures of central tendency are statistical measures that represent the center or typical value of a dataset. The three main measures are Mean, Median, and Mode.

    Formula:


    1. Mean (Average): Sum of all values ÷ Number of values

    2. Median: Middle value when data is ordered

    3. Mode: Most frequently occurring value

    Example 1: Mean Calculation

    Calculate the mean of test scores: 85, 92, 78, 90, 95

    Solution:

    Sum of scores: 85 + 92 + 78 + 90 + 95 = 440

    Number of scores: 5

    Mean = 440 ÷ 5 = 88

    Example 2: Median and Mode

    Find the median and mode of: 3, 7, 5, 5, 8, 7, 3

    Solution:

    Ordered data: 3, 3, 5, 5, 7, 7, 8

    Median: Middle value = 5

    Mode: Most frequent values = 3 and 5 (bimodal)

    Fundamental Counting Principles

    The Fundamental Counting Principle helps determine the total number of possible outcomes when multiple independent events occur.

    Formula:


    Total Outcomes = (Number of ways first event can occur) × (Number of ways second event can occur) × ...

    Example 1: Clothing Combinations

    You have 3 shirts, 2 pants, and 4 shoes. How many different outfits can you make?

    Solution:

    Shirts: 3 options

    Pants: 2 options

    Shoes: 4 options

    Total combinations: 3 × 2 × 4 = 24 different outfits

    Example 2: Car Customization

    A car model offers 5 color choices, 3 interior options, and 2 wheel designs. How many unique car configurations are possible?

    Solution:

    Color choices: 5 options

    Interior options: 3 options

    Wheel designs: 2 options

    Total configurations: 5 × 3 × 2 = 30 unique car designs

    Permutations and Combinations

    Understanding Permutations

    A permutation is an arrangement of objects where order matters. It represents the number of ways to arrange a set of items when the sequence is important. Permutations are used in scheduling, password generation, and event organization.

    Permutation Formula:


    P(n,r) = n! / (n-r)!

    where n is the total number of items and r is the number of items being arranged

    Example 1: Arranging Books

    If you have 5 different books and want to arrange 3 of them on a shelf, how many different arrangements are possible?

    Solution:

    n = 5 (total books)

    r = 3 (books to arrange)

    P(5,3) = 5! / (5-3)!

    P(5,3) = 5! / 2! = (5 × 4 × 3) = 60 different arrangements

    Example 2: PIN Numbers

    How many different 3-digit PIN numbers can be created using the digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 without repeating any digit?

    Solution:

    n = 5 (available digits)

    r = 3 (PIN length)

    P(5,3) = 5! / (5-3)! = 60 different PIN numbers

    Understanding Combinations

    A combination is a selection of items where the order does not matter. It represents the number of ways to choose elements from a larger group without considering the sequence. Combinations are often used in statistics, lottery draws, and team formations.

    Combination Formula:


    C(n,r) = n! / (r! × (n-r)!)

    where n is the total number of items and r is the number of items being selected

    Example 1: Team Selection

    From a group of 10 students, how many different ways can you select 3 students for a project team?

    Solution:

    n = 10 (total students)

    r = 3 (team size)

    C(10,3) = 10! / (3! × (10-3)!)

    C(10,3) = 10! / (3! × 7!) = 120 different possible teams

    Example 2: Card Game

    In a standard deck of 52 cards, how many different 5-card hands are possible?

    Solution:

    n = 52 (total cards)

    r = 5 (cards in hand)

    C(52,5) = 52! / (5! × 47!) = 2,598,960 possible hands

    Probability

    What is Probability?

    Probability is a way of quantifying the likelihood of an event happening. It ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain) and indicates how likely an outcome is. Probability theory plays a key role in fields like statistics, weather prediction, gambling, and risk analysis.

    Formula:


    P(event) = Number of Favorable Outcomes / Total Number of Possible Outcomes

    Example 1: Rolling a Die

    What is the probability of rolling an even number on a standard six-sided die?

    Solution:

    Favorable outcomes: 2, 4, 6 (three even numbers)

    Total possible outcomes: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 (six numbers)

    P(even) = 3/6 = 1/2 = 0.5 or 50%

    Example 2: Drawing Cards

    What is the probability of drawing a heart from a standard deck of 52 cards?

    Solution:

    Favorable outcomes: 13 hearts

    Total possible outcomes: 52 cards

    P(heart) = 13/52 = 1/4 = 0.25 or 25%
